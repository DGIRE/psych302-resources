A Message to You, Before We Begin

This course has an unusual opening promise: when you finish it, you will not understand how the brain works. Nobody does. After more than a century of modern neuroscience we still have no complete account of how physical brain activity produces thoughts, experiences, intentions, memories, or the feeling of being a self. I will not pretend otherwise.

What this course will do is give you a genuinely new and more accurate way of thinking about the brain. The core idea: the brain does not passively receive information from the world — it actively predicts what it expects to encounter, and updates those predictions when it is wrong.

This is not easy material. The course spans philosophy of mind, brain systems, and computational modeling rarely encountered in a psychology curriculum. I will give you the scaffolding you need and meet you where you are. But I will not water it down, because the ideas are too important.

“The aspects of the mind that feel most fundamental — the sense of self, the experience of choosing, what you believe, what you feel, your moral intuitions — are not fixed structures in the brain. They are patterns of prediction and habit that evolved to make a staggeringly complex world navigable.”

Course Architecture

PSYCH 302 is a structured argument about how the brain produces the mind — not a collection of independent topics. The lectures are organized into two main parts, bookended by Foundations at the start.

Part 1 — The Systems (L3–10): we examine the inner workings of neural systems — language, sensation, attention, emotion, memory, and cognitive control.

Part 2 — The Whole (L11–17): we establish how these systems interact to create a unified whole. The arc moves through agency, Global Workspace Theory, the predictive and dynamic brain, the prosocial brain, the constructed self, and culminates with altered states of consciousness.

Your Study Strategy
1
Front-load Weeks 1–3. Lectures 3–5 introduce the anatomical vocabulary the entire course depends on. Draw brain networks by hand. Make flashcards. Spend more time here than you think you need.
2
Use Lecture 6 as a running anchor. After each subsequent lecture, ask: where does today’s topic sit relative to the three levels of processing (subliminal / preconscious / conscious)?
3
Prepare early for Lectures 12–14. These are the steepest three lectures. Watch short videos on Bayesian inference and dynamical systems before L13–14. Use office hours.
4
After every lecture, update your mental map. Take 5 minutes to write what new concept was introduced, which earlier lecture it connects to, and what it prepares you for next.
5
Track the five concept threads: Distributed vs. localized; consciousness signatures; top-down vs. bottom-up; clinical applications; the nature of the self.
