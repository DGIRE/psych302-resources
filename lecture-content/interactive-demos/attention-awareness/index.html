1. Three Levels of Processing

Dehaene et al. (2006) proposed that sensory stimuli are not simply "seen" or "unseen." Instead, processing falls into three distinct levels, determined by two independent factors: stimulus strength (bottom-up signal) and attentional amplification (top-down).

2. The Ignition Simulator

In Dehaene's Global Neuronal Workspace model, conscious perception occurs when a sensory signal triggers ignition — a sudden, nonlinear surge of activity across parietal and prefrontal cortex. Two conditions must be met: the signal must be strong enough, and top-down attention must amplify it. Adjust both sliders to see when the brain "ignites."

The key insight: Ignition is all-or-nothing. A stimulus just below the threshold produces only local sensory activity. One tiny step above the threshold, and activity suddenly broadcasts across the entire frontoparietal network — consciousness switches on like a light.

3. ERP Signatures of Conscious Access

Event-related potentials (ERPs) are voltage changes recorded from scalp electrodes, time-locked to a stimulus. Different ERP components arise from different brain regions and at different latencies — and they tell us exactly when processing transitions from subliminal to preconscious to conscious.

The critical finding: Early components (C1, P1, N1) appear whether or not a stimulus reaches consciousness — they reflect automatic sensory processing. The P3b (~300–500ms), recorded over parietal cortex, is the electrophysiological signature of ignition: it appears only for consciously perceived stimuli and is absent for both subliminal and preconscious ones.

4. Inattentional Blindness

Simons & Chabris (1999) showed that when attention is fully engaged on a task, people fail to notice a clearly visible, unexpected event — even a person in a gorilla suit walking through the scene. This is inattentional blindness: the stimulus is strong (it's right there!), but attention is consumed elsewhere.

Connection to Dehaene's model: In inattentional blindness the gorilla stimulus is strong (not subliminal), but attention is fully deployed to the counting task. The gorilla remains in a preconscious state — it has sufficient signal strength to reach consciousness, but without attentional amplification, it never ignites in the global workspace.

5. Change Blindness

In the flicker paradigm, two versions of a scene alternate with a brief blank flash between them. Even large changes — a building disappearing, a color swap — can go undetected for many seconds. The blank disrupts the motion transient that would normally draw attention to the change.

Why it works: Without the transient signal from abrupt change (the blank resets it), you must serially search the scene with focused attention. Each location you're not attending to remains in a preconscious state — the change is there, but without attention directed to that location, it cannot enter awareness.

6. The Attentional Blink

When two targets appear in a rapid stream, detecting the first target (T1) causes a brief "blink" during which the second target (T2) is often missed — especially if it appears 200–500ms after T1. This attentional blink reveals a processing bottleneck: T1's entry into the global workspace temporarily blocks T2 from achieving ignition.

Dehaene's interpretation: Processing T1 occupies the global workspace. T2 arrives while the workspace is still broadcasting T1 — there are no resources left to amplify T2. It remains preconscious and decays before attention becomes available again. By ~700ms, the workspace has cleared and T2 can ignite normally.

7. Putting It All Together

Each phenomenon maps onto Dehaene's taxonomy. The same underlying mechanism — whether attention amplifies a signal into the global workspace — explains why stimuli can be physically present yet psychologically invisible.

8. Check Your Understanding

1. According to Dehaene et al. (2006), what two conditions must both be met for a stimulus to become conscious?

Dehaene's taxonomy is built on two independent factors: bottom-up signal strength and top-down attentional amplification. Both are required for ignition in the global neuronal workspace, which produces conscious perception.

2. In Dehaene's framework, a preconscious stimulus is one that:

A preconscious stimulus has enough strength to potentially reach consciousness — it is not subliminal. What it lacks is attention. If attention were redirected to it, it could ignite in the global workspace and become conscious. The gorilla in inattentional blindness is preconscious, not subliminal.

3. Why does inattentional blindness provide evidence for the role of attention in conscious access?

The gorilla is large, clearly visible, and present for several seconds — it has strong bottom-up signal strength. Yet roughly 50% of participants miss it entirely because their attention is locked onto the counting task. This dissociation proves that stimulus strength alone cannot produce consciousness; attentional amplification is also required.

4. What is "ignition" in the Global Neuronal Workspace model?

Ignition is the hallmark of conscious access: a sudden, all-or-nothing cascade where parietal and prefrontal neurons become synchronized and broadcast the stimulus representation to the entire cortex. It is nonlinear — activity doesn't gradually increase; it either ignites or it doesn't. This is associated with the P3b ERP component and late gamma-band synchrony.

5. During the attentional blink, T2 is missed because:

T2 has strong signal strength (it's clearly visible and processed by early sensory areas — early ERPs are present). However, T1 is still occupying the global workspace, consuming the top-down attentional resources needed for ignition. T2 arrives in a "preconscious" state and decays before the workspace clears. By ~700ms (Lag 7+), T1 processing has finished and T2 can ignite normally.
