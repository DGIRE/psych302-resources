Two Visions of the Brain

Classical cognitive science treated the brain as a largely feedforward feature-detection device: sensory signals travel "up" through increasingly abstract processing stages until a percept is formed. The predictive processing framework inverts this picture — the brain is primarily in the business of generating top-down predictions and only passing forward the residual prediction errors that those predictions fail to explain.

Precision Weighting & Attention

Not all prediction errors are created equal. The brain assigns precision (inverse variance) to different error signals based on their estimated reliability. High-precision errors carry more weight and drive stronger updates. This precision-weighting mechanism provides a natural account of attention: attending to something means boosting the precision (gain) on error units in the relevant processing stream.

Experiment with the sliders: When prior strength is high and sensory precision is low, perception is dominated by expectations — you "see" what you expect. Clinical Implications

Disruptions in precision weighting may underlie several psychiatric and neurological conditions. If the brain consistently assigns too much precision to prediction errors (treating noise as signal), this could contribute to the perceptual disturbances seen in schizophrenia — where ordinary inputs feel deeply surprising and laden with false significance. Conversely, if sensory precision is chronically reduced, the system becomes overly reliant on prior expectations, potentially contributing to features of autism spectrum conditions where reduced context-sensitivity and enhanced detail-processing have been documented.

Active Inference & the Free-Energy Principle

Perhaps the most radical implication of predictive processing is its extension to action. In classical models, perception and action are separate systems — you perceive the world, then decide what to do. In the predictive processing framework, perception and action are two sides of the same coin: both work to minimize prediction error.

Two Ways to Minimize Prediction Error

The Desert Landscape Scenario

Imagine you are a creature in a desert. You predict cool water ahead. There is a prediction error — the world is hot and dry. You can resolve this two ways:

Perception (update model)

Revise your expectation — accept you are in a desert. The internal model changes to match reality.

Action (change world)

Walk to an oasis. Move your body so sensory input matches your prediction of finding water.

In the predictive processing framework, these are computationally equivalent — both reduce prediction error. Motor commands are simply proprioceptive predictions: the brain "predicts" a limb position, and the motor system acts to make it true.

The deep unification: Perception, action, attention, learning, and even emotion all emerge from a single imperative — minimize prediction error across all levels of the hierarchy, across all timescales. This is what Friston's free-energy principle attempts to formalize: every aspect of brain function (and perhaps all adaptive biological systems) can be understood as free-energy minimization.

Check Your Understanding

Test your grasp of key predictive processing concepts from Clark (2013).

1. In the predictive processing framework, what is the primary information carried by forward (bottom-up) connections?

In predictive processing, forward connections carry prediction errors — the mismatch between what was predicted by higher areas and what actually arrived. This is a fundamental departure from feedforward models where the full sensory signal travels upward. As Clark describes, backward connections do the "heavy lifting" by carrying predictions downward.

2. How does the predictive processing account explain the role of attention?

In predictive processing, attention is understood as precision weighting — adjusting the gain on prediction error units. Attending to something means assigning high precision to the relevant error signals, making them more influential in updating the brain's generative model. This is achieved by altering the "volume" on error units at different levels.

3. What does "active inference" refer to in the predictive processing framework?

Active inference extends predictive processing to action: rather than only updating internal models (perception), the brain can also change the world through motor action so that incoming sensory signals match existing predictions. Proprioceptive predictions become motor commands — the system "predicts" a body state and the motor system acts to fulfill it.

4. According to Clark, what makes hierarchical predictive processing fundamentally different from a simple top-down "expectation" model?

The key innovation of hierarchical predictive processing is its bidirectional, multi-level architecture: predictions flow downward and prediction errors flow upward at every level simultaneously. This creates a generative model where each level tries to predict activity at the level below. It is not simply "expectations influence perception" — it is a complete computational architecture where backward connections carry the main processing load.

5. The free-energy principle (Friston) proposes that:

The free-energy principle provides a unifying mathematical framework: all adaptive biological systems (from cells to brains) act to minimize variational free energy, which serves as an upper bound on sensory surprise. In the brain, this manifests as prediction error minimization via perception (updating models), action (changing the world), and attention (weighting errors by precision).
