2. The Speed Race: How Fast Is Each Pathway?

The three pathways operate at dramatically different speeds. The subcortical route is the fastest because it takes the shortest path and bypasses cortical processing entirely. The dorsal stream prioritizes speed for guiding actions. The ventral stream is slowest because it performs the deepest analysis.

Why does speed matter? When a snake appears at your feet, your body jumps before you consciously see it. The subcortical route triggers your amygdala in ~50ms. Your dorsal stream guides the jump at ~80ms. Your ventral stream finally tells you "that's a snake" at ~150ms. Three pathways, three timescales, one survival response.

3. See the Difference: Dorsal vs. Ventral in Action

Watch a ball fly across the screen. The dorsal stream immediately tracks its motion and location — you know where it is going. The ventral stream takes longer but gradually resolves what the object is: its color, texture, pattern, and identity.

The key insight: The dorsal stream extracts motion and spatial information almost instantly via fast feedforward processing. The ventral stream requires time-consuming recurrent processing to build a detailed representation. You can track and dodge a ball before you consciously recognize what it looks like.

4. Signal Timeline: What Happens When

When light hits your retina, a cascade of processing unfolds over 200 milliseconds. Watch how the three pathways produce different outcomes at different times.

0 ms — Light hits retina

Photoreceptors transduce light into electrical signals. The signal immediately splits: ~90% goes to the LGN, ~10% goes directly to the superior colliculus.

~20 ms — Superior colliculus activates

The subcortical route reaches the midbrain first. The superior colliculus begins processing spatial location and motion — no cortex needed.

~30 ms — LGN relays signal

The lateral geniculate nucleus passes the signal to primary visual cortex (V1). This is a relay station, not just a passive wire — it filters and gates information.

~50 ms — Amygdala fires (subcortical route)

Via the pulvinar, the amygdala receives a coarse but fast threat assessment. You flinch at a looming shape before you know what it is. This is the neural basis of "gut feelings" about danger.

~60 ms — V1 feedforward sweep

V1 completes its initial feedforward pass. Orientation, spatial frequency, and motion are extracted. The signal begins diverging into dorsal and ventral streams.

~80 ms — Dorsal stream: action guidance ready

MT/V5 and the posterior parietal cortex compute spatial location and motion direction. You can begin reaching for an object or dodging a threat — your hand "knows" the shape before you consciously identify it.

~100 ms — Recurrent processing begins

Feedback signals from higher areas re-enter V1 and V2. According to Lamme & Roelfsema (2000), this recurrent processing is necessary for conscious visual experience — feedforward alone is not enough.

~150 ms — Ventral stream: object recognition

Inferotemporal (IT) cortex completes object identification. You now consciously know what you're looking at — a face, a snake, a coffee cup. This is the slow, detailed analysis that the ventral "what" pathway provides.

~200 ms — Unified percept

All pathways converge. You have a coherent, conscious experience combining identity (ventral), location and action plans (dorsal), and emotional valence (subcortical). But your body has already been acting for 150ms.

5. The Blindsight Task

Patient D.B. (studied by Weiskrantz, 1996) had damage to V1 in one hemisphere, leaving him cortically blind in the opposite visual field. Yet when forced to guess, he could point to objects he insisted he could not see. This is blindsight — vision without visual awareness.

How is this possible? The subcortical pathway (retina → superior colliculus → pulvinar) is intact. It provides enough spatial information for orienting and basic detection — but without V1 feeding the ventral stream, there is no conscious perception. The patient responds to stimuli they genuinely cannot see.
