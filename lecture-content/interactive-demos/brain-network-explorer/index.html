Core Framework

The Sensory-Fugal Hierarchy: Five Cortical Zones

Mesulam's central insight is that the cerebral cortex can be divided into five functional zones arranged along a gradient from the most highly differentiated primary sensory–motor areas to the least differentiated limbic structures. Information flows "sensory-fugally" (away from sensation) through these zones, with each step adding complexity and abstraction.

Connectivity Architecture

How Information Flows: The Concentric Ring Model

Mesulam represents the cortex as concentric rings (Figure 2 in the paper), where each ring corresponds to a different synaptic level. Level 1 is occupied by primary sensory cortex (e.g., V1). Each successive ring is separated by at least one synaptic step. Information flows outward from sensation to cognition through both serial and parallel pathways.

Large-Scale Networks

Five Neurocognitive Networks

Mesulam identifies at least five large-scale distributed networks, each organized around interconnected transmodal epicentres. These networks are not isolated modules—they share components and individual cortical nodes can dynamically shift their network affiliation depending on the task.

Spatial Awareness

Key Concept

Transmodal Gateways and Multimodal Binding

Transmodal areas are the highest synaptic levels of sensory-fugal processing. They receive input from multiple unimodal areas and serve as critical gateways (not storage centres) for binding modality-specific fragments into coherent experiences, memories, and thoughts. Think of them as directories or lookup tables that point to where information is stored, rather than warehouses that contain the information itself.

HOW BINDING WORKS (FIGURE 3 CONCEPT)

When two unimodal areas (A and B) are simultaneously active—say the visual form of a face and the sound of a voice—their co-activation is registered by a transmodal node (Tr) through Hebbian covariance. The transmodal node does not store a merged copy. Instead, it creates a directory entry that links A and B. Later, activating A alone is enough to reactivate B through Tr, enabling associative retrieval and recognition.

This "binding through directories" model explains why transmodal lesions cause global multimodal deficits (the directory is lost), while unimodal lesions cause modality-specific deficits (one entry is lost, but the rest remain).

Top-Down Processing

How the Brain Sculpts Sensory Experience

Information doesn't just flow bottom-up from sensation to cognition. Higher transmodal areas exert powerful top-down influences that reshape activity at earlier synaptic levels. Mesulam describes several kinds of modulation that sculpt the raw sensory signal into a subjectively meaningful experience.
